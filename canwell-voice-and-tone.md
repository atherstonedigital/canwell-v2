## What this brief does

This document locks how Canwell Interiors speaks: on the website, in emails, in ads, in social, in GBP posts, in shop signage, anywhere customers read words attached to the brand.

It is not the copy itself. It is the brief the copy is written against.

Two tests for any sentence written under this brief:

1. Does it sound like Gary or Gemma would say it standing in the showroom?
2. Would a customer in Willersey understand it on the first read?

If the answer to either is no, rewrite.

---

## Strategic recap

- **Positioning:** *The Cotswolds furnishings showroom. Walk in, choose, take home.*
- **Brand architecture:** Canwell is the showroom with helpful advice. Saverys is the bespoke design house. The voice should never blur the two.
- **Customer job:** Prove the showroom is worth a weekend visit.
- **Audience:** Locals and surrounding villages, across five segments, weekend-led.

The voice exists to serve these. Anything that pulls against them is wrong, no matter how well-written.

---

## The voice in one sentence

> Canwell sounds like the friendly expert in a real shop, not a brochure.

Warm, plainspoken, practical, locally rooted, and honestly helpful. Confident about what it stocks and what it can do. Never reaching for "luxury" language, never pretending to be more than a family-run showroom that happens to be very good at what it does.

---

## The six principles

### 1. Plain English. Always.

Short sentences. Anglo-Saxon words where possible. One idea per sentence. If a sentence needs to be read twice, rewrite it.

A customer in Willersey thinking about new curtains is not impressed by "curated", "bespoke", or "ethos." They want to know what's in stock, what it costs roughly, and when they can come and see it.

### 2. Lead with the reader, not the brand

Every page opens by addressing the reader's question, not by announcing what Canwell does. "Looking for a sofa you can actually sit on before you buy?" beats "Canwell Interiors is proud to offer a fine selection of premium sofas."

The pronoun balance should run roughly two-to-one in favour of "you" over "we."

### 3. Specific, not abstract

Name the village. Name the brand. Name the service. "Free measure across the Cotswolds, from Chipping Campden to Cheltenham" beats "comprehensive geographic coverage."

If a sentence could be on any furniture website in the UK, it doesn't belong on Canwell's. Local specificity is the trust signal.

### 4. Honest about what things are

A made-to-measure curtain is a made-to-measure curtain. Not a "bespoke window dressing solution." A free in-store consultation is a free in-store consultation. Not a "complimentary design experience."

Plain naming is the differentiator from the design-house category Canwell isn't competing in.

### 5. Confident, never boastful

State what's true. Don't reach for "incredible", "stunning", "exceptional." If the showroom has a thousand fabric samples, say "over a thousand fabric samples." If it has been in Broadway twenty years, say that. Specifics over superlatives, every time.

### 6. Warmly informal, never cloying

The tone is the friend who runs a good shop, not the corporate marketer pretending to be a friend. "Come in and we'll help you sort it out" is on-brand. "We're passionate about creating spaces you'll love" is not.

## Voice in action

Three short reference samples. These set the bar for every paragraph produced in copy production.

### Homepage hero

> **The Cotswolds furnishings showroom.**
> **Walk in, choose, take home.**
>
> On Broadway High Street, with carpets, curtains, blinds, furniture, and the kind of design help you actually want. Family-run, open seven days, free parking out the front.

### Service hub (carpets)

> Choosing a carpet is one of the bigger calls you'll make in a room. Best done in person, in good light, with the rest of your scheme in mind.
>
> Come in and we'll show you the range, talk through what works in older Cotswold houses, take measurements, and quote you honestly. The measure is free. So is the second opinion.

### Design help

> Most people who walk in for help don't want a project. They want someone who knows what they're doing to look at their room, listen to what they're trying to do, and tell them what would actually work.
>
> That's what this is. Free, unfussy, no commission on what you choose. Bring photos, dimensions if you have them, and the curtain you're trying to match.

Each example does the same thing: opens with the reader's question, names the service plainly, and gives a reason to come in.

## Vocabulary: words we don't use

Banned. If any of these appear in a draft, rewrite the sentence.

**Interior-design jargon:**
bespoke (unless naming a service), curate, curated, vision, ethos, story (the brand has none), aesthetic, design philosophy, signature style, statement piece, design-led, design-forward, design ethos, atmosphere (as a deliverable).

**Faux-luxury:**
luxury, luxurious, exquisite, sumptuous, opulent, refined, sophisticated, premium (sparingly OK on brand stockist pages, never as a headline), discerning, elevated, elevate, the finest, the very best, world-class.

**Tourist-board Cotswolds:**
nestled, picturesque, idyllic, quintessential, charming (overused), chocolate-box, the heart of, hidden gem.

**Corporate fluff:**
we are passionate about, we strive to, our commitment is, we pride ourselves on, dedicated to, delivering exceptional, customer-centric, end-to-end, holistic, comprehensive, seamless, leverage, robust, dynamic.

**AI tells:**
delve, dive into, navigate (as metaphor), tapestry, landscape (as metaphor), realm, pivotal, underscore, foster, testament, harness, illuminate, unlock, vibrant, rich (metaphorical), in today's fast-paced world, in an ever-evolving market.

**Sales-speak:**
incredible savings, unbeatable, must-see, today only, don't miss out, exclusive, limited edition (unless it actually is).

When in doubt, ask: would a sensible person say this out loud in the showroom? If no, cut.

## Tone variations by page type

The voice is the same everywhere. The tone shifts slightly. Same person, different conversations.

| Page type | Tone leans | Example opening |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage | Welcoming, confident | "The Cotswolds furnishings showroom. Walk in, choose, take home." |
| Visit us | Clear, practical | "We're on Kennel Lane in Broadway, open seven days." |
| About | Personal, warm | "Canwell has been on Broadway High Street for [X] years. Gary and Gemma run the place." |
| Service hubs | Practical, expert | "Choosing a carpet is one of the bigger calls you'll make in a room." |
| Service subs (MTM) | Reassuring, process-led | "Made-to-measure works like this..." |
| Design help | Warm, defusing | "Most people who walk in for help don't want a project." |
| Brand stockist | Confident, factual | "Richmond Interiors at Canwell. Sofas, dining, and accessories on the showroom floor." |
| Location pages | Practical, neighbourly | "Twenty minutes from Cheltenham, on the way to Stratford." |
| Inspiration | Conversational, opinionated | "Cotswold cottage windows are a particular puzzle. Most are tall, narrow, and in stone reveals that don't play nicely with standard tracks." |

The biggest tone risk is the **design help** page. If the tone reads "posh designer", we've failed. It must read "the helpful expert in the showroom."

## Punctuation and formatting rules

| Rule | Practice |
|---|---|
| Em dashes | Never. Use commas, full stops, colons, or parentheses. |
| En dashes | Never except in numeric ranges (£100 to £500, not £100-£500). |
| Sentence case in headings | Yes. "Made-to-measure curtains" not "Made-To-Measure Curtains." |
| Capitalisation | British: only proper nouns and start of sentence. |
| Oxford comma | Use it. (Carpets, curtains, and blinds.) |
| Quotation marks | Single quotes ('like this'). Double quotes for quotes within quotes. |
| Numbers | Words for one to nine, numerals for 10+. Exceptions for measurements, prices, and addresses. |
| Pounds | £100, not 100GBP or £100.00. |
| Telephone | UK format: 01905 964994, not (01905) 964994 or +44 1905 964994 (unless international context). |
| Postcode | WR12 7DJ. Always uppercase, single space. |
| Web | xshowhome.com, canwellinteriors.com (lowercase, no www). |

## Patterns to avoid (the AI-tell list)

These structural patterns flag copy as machine-written. Cut on sight.

1. **Negative parallelism.** "It's not just X, it's Y." "This isn't a sofa, it's a sanctuary." Cut.
2. **Rule of three by default.** "Beautiful, timeless, and practical." Trim to one or two.
3. **Trailing puffery.** "...marking a new chapter in Cotswold interiors." Delete the participle phrase.
4. **False ranges.** "From small homes to grand estates." Unless both ends are real.
5. **Heritage / tourist-board language.** "Boasts a rich heritage." Strip the adjectives.
6. **Bulleted bold-titled lists where prose would work.** "**Quality:** We choose well." Either make it real prose or use plain bullets.
7. **Editorialising on facts.** "Interestingly, the showroom..." State the fact, let the reader judge.
8. **Sycophancy and scaffolding.** "We're delighted to..." "Here at Canwell..." Get on with it.
9. **Elegant variation.** Don't call Canwell "the showroom" then "the boutique" then "the destination." It's Canwell. Or the showroom. Pick one and stick with it.
10. **Compulsive summaries at the end of pages.** Stop when you're done.
